## Monday — the_strategy_pitch
Your ICP isn't a demographic. It's a psychological profile.

Most founders build their ICP like a census form: industry, company size, revenue range. Then they wonder why outbound feels like screaming into the void.

Here's the framework shift:

The best ICPs are built around a *decision trigger*, not a data point.

I worked with a B2B founder selling compliance software. His ICP was "mid-market fintech companies." Broad. Useless. Response rates were under 1%.

We rebuilt his ICP around a trigger: companies that had just received their first regulatory warning letter. Suddenly he wasn't selling software. He was selling relief from existential dread.

Response rates jumped to 11%. Pipeline tripled in 60 days.

The framework:
1. Identify the *event* that creates urgency
2. Map the *emotion* that event produces
3. Position your solution as the bridge from pain to resolution
4. Build your list around the trigger, not the firmographic

Your ICP should make you feel uncomfortable with how specific it is. If it doesn't, it's not specific enough.

## Wednesday — axis_chamber
You don't have a pricing problem. You have a conviction problem.

Inside Axis Chamber, we track a metric most people ignore: how many times a founder discounts before being asked to.

The number is staggering. Over 60% of the founders we onboard are preemptively discounting — dropping price in the proposal before the prospect even pushes back.

One member, a fractional CFO, was charging $4,500/month. Good work. Strong results. But every time he sent a proposal, he'd add "introductory pricing" language. His close rate? 22%.

We ran an experiment. Same offer. $7,500/month. No discount language. Added a 90-day performance clause instead — if benchmarks aren't hit, client can exit.

Close rate jumped to 35%.

Higher price. Higher close rate. Why? Because price communicates certainty. When you discount before being asked, you're telling the buyer: "I'm not sure this is worth it either."

The system we use:
→ Set the price at your conviction level
→ Remove all apologetic language
→ Add a risk-reversal mechanism

## Thursday — the_strategy_pitch
The most dangerous GTM mistake: building positioning around your product instead of your buyer's identity.

Salesforce didn't win because they had better CRM features in 2004. They won because they made "No Software" an identity. They turned their buyer into a rebel — someone too smart, too modern for clunky on-premise systems.

Identity-based positioning is the highest leverage GTM move a founder can make. And almost no one does it.

Here's why it works psychologically: people don't buy solutions. They buy versions of themselves. Your positioning should answer: "What does my buyer *become* when they choose us?"

Three-step identity positioning framework:
1. Name the OLD identity your buyer is stuck in ("You're still running GTM on gut feel")
2. Name the NEW identity your solution enables ("Data-driven founders who scale predictably")
3. Make the gap between them feel urgent and emotional

A founder I advise in the HR tech space repositioned from "AI-powered recruiting" to "the hiring system for founders who refuse to make another bad hire." Pipeline velocity increased 40% in one quarter.

Your buyer doesn't want features. They want to become someone.
